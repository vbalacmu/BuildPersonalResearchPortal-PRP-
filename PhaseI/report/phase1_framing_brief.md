# Phase 1 Framing Brief


**Domain:** Job policy, regulation, AI/automation, and Universal Basic Income

---

## Main Research Question

AI and automation are widely seen as threats to employment, but estimates of job loss and displacement vary sharply—from modest sectoral shifts to large-scale disruption. In response, policymakers and researchers have put forward competing ideas: universal basic income (UBI) or guaranteed income to cushion displacement; retraining and wage subsidies to help workers transition; and regulation of AI in hiring and the workplace (e.g., EU AI Act) to limit harm. This project asks how that debate is framed, what evidence is marshaled for and against different policies, and where sources agree or disagree on the scale of impact and the best policy response. The goal is a grounded synthesis of the literature that supports citation-backed answers and clear identification of gaps and disagreements.

How do policymakers and researchers link AI and automation to job displacement, and what role do proposals like UBI and related policy (retraining, safety nets, regulation) play in the debate?


---

## Sub-Questions (Retrievable Parts)

1. How is "job displacement" or "employment impact" from AI/automation defined and measured in policy and economics literature?

2. What empirical evidence exists on automation and job loss or job change (sectors, time periods, methods)?

3. How is UBI (or similar income guarantees) argued for or against as a response to automation?

4. What other policy responses are proposed (retraining, wage subsidies, regulation of AI in hiring, etc.) and how do they compare?

5. How do regulatory or legislative proposals (e.g. EU AI Act, US state laws) address AI and work?

6. Where do sources agree or disagree on the scale of impact and the best policy response?

---

## Scope

The corpus for this project is bounded by two concerns: relevance to the main question (AI/automation, jobs, and policy responses such as UBI and retraining) and verifiability (sources that make claims we can cite and check). We include work that defines or measures job displacement, argues for or against UBI or other policy levers, or describes regulatory approaches to AI and work. We favor post-2015 material so that the synthesis reflects the current wave of AI and automation debate rather than earlier automation literatures. We exclude purely technical AI papers, unsupported opinion, and speculative futures that are not anchored in existing studies or policy.

### In Scope:
- Policy reports, economic studies, legislative/regulatory proposals, and peer-reviewed work on automation, AI, and labor markets (including job displacement, wage effects, skill requirements, UBI, retraining, and regulation)
- Both empirical studies and normative/policy arguments with cited evidence
- Academic papers, government reports, think tank analyses, and regulatory documents
- Focus on post-2015 research (recent AI/automation wave)

### Out of Scope:
- Pure technical AI papers with no policy or labor angle
- Opinion pieces with no cited evidence
- Futures/speculation without grounding in existing studies or policy
- Historical automation studies pre-2015
